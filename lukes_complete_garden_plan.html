Big Beef

Growing Profile

  • Days to maturity: 73 days
  • Plant type: Indeterminate
  • Fruit size: 10-16 oz
  • Sun: Full sun (6-8 hrs)
  • Spacing: 24-36" apart
  • Disease resistance: VFNTASt (excellent)

Hybrid — Not recommended

Learn More

  • Watch: Growing Big Beef TomatoesSearch YouTube
  • Learn: Pruning indeterminate tomatoes for max yield ↓ details
    Why prune indeterminate tomatoes?

    Indeterminate tomatoes (like Big Beef) grow continuously all season. Without pruning, they put energy into foliage instead of fruit. Pruning redirects energy into fewer, larger, better-tasting tomatoes and improves airflow to prevent disease.

    What are suckers & where to find them

    Suckers are small shoots that grow in the "V" joint (axil) between the main stem and a branch. Left alone, each sucker becomes a full new stem that flowers and fruits — but later and smaller.

    S = Sucker growing in the V-joint. Pinch or snap these off when small (under 4").

    How to prune — step by step
    • When: Start when plant is 12-18" tall. Check weekly.
    • Below first flower cluster: Remove ALL suckers below the first flower truss. These will never produce fruit worth keeping.
    • Above first flower cluster: You have a choice — remove all suckers for fewer but larger fruit, or leave 1-2 suckers to become secondary stems for more (smaller) fruit.
    • Missouri pruning: Instead of removing the whole sucker, pinch off just the tip. This leaves some foliage for sun protection while stopping rampant growth. Good for Decatur's intense summer sun.
    • Late season: In August, "top" the plant by cutting the main growing tip. This forces remaining energy into ripening existing fruit before fall.
    For Big Beef specifically: Allow 1-2 main stems max. Big Beef produces large fruit (10-16 oz) and needs energy concentrated. Remove suckers weekly.
    ▶ Watch: Pruning indeterminate tomatoes (YouTube) 📖 UGA Extension: Growing Tomatoes in Georgia
  • Understand: Why hybrids don't save true to seed ↓ details
    What is an F1 hybrid?

    An F1 hybrid is the first-generation cross between two genetically distinct parent lines. Breeders cross Parent A (e.g., great disease resistance) with Parent B (e.g., huge fruit) to get offspring that have both traits. This is called hybrid vigor.

    Why seeds from hybrids are unpredictable

    When you save seeds from an F1 hybrid like Big Beef, the offspring (F2 generation) are a genetic shuffle. Some will resemble Parent A, some Parent B, and most will be something in between — often worse than either parent. You might get:

    • Small fruit instead of large
    • Lost disease resistance
    • Different flavor, color, or growth habit
    • Reduced yield
    What to do instead
    • For hybrids (Big Beef, Whopper, Lunchbox): Buy new seed/transplants each year
    • For open-pollinated/heirloom (Brandywine, Cherokee Purple, Roma): Save seeds freely! They grow true
    Your garden has both types. Save seeds from your heirlooms (Brandywine, Cherokee, Yellow Pear, Roma) and buy fresh seed for hybrids (Big Beef, Whopper, Sweet 150).

  • Learn: Cross-pollination isolation for heirloom purity ↓ details
    Do tomatoes cross-pollinate?

    Yes, but rarely. Tomatoes are primarily self-pollinating — each flower pollinates itself before it fully opens. Cross-pollination by bees happens 2-5% of the time in most gardens.

    Isolation for pure seed
    • Casual saving: 10 ft between varieties is usually fine. You'll get 95%+ true seed.
    • Strict purity: 25-50 ft between varieties, or bag individual flower clusters with organza bags before they open.
    • Your garden: With 8 tomato varieties in Bed A, some crossing is possible. For your first year, just save from your most isolated plant (Rainbow at the trellis end) and see how the offspring look.
    Cross-pollination only affects the seeds inside the fruit, not the fruit itself. A crossed Rainbow tomato still looks and tastes like Rainbow — the surprise shows up next year when you grow the seeds.
    ▶ Watch: Tomato isolation for seed saving
  • Understand: Low-acid tomatoes and safe canning ↓ details
    Why acid matters for canning

    Safe water-bath canning requires a pH of 4.6 or below. Most red tomatoes are right at the edge (pH 4.3-4.6). Low-acid varieties like Rainbow can be above 4.6, which means Clostridium botulinum bacteria could survive.

    The fix: always add acid
    • Per quart: Add 2 tablespoons bottled lemon juice OR 1/2 teaspoon citric acid
    • Per pint: Add 1 tablespoon lemon juice OR 1/4 teaspoon citric acid
    • This is recommended for ALL tomato varieties, not just low-acid ones
    • Use bottled lemon juice (consistent acidity), not fresh
    For your Rainbow tomatoes: Always add acid when canning. For fresh jam that you freeze (not can), acidity doesn't matter for safety — the freezer handles preservation.
    📖 USDA/UGA: Safe tomato canning guide

  • Learn: Preventing blossom end rot and cracking ↓ details
    Blossom End Rot (BER)

    Dark, sunken, leathery patch on the bottom of the fruit. Caused by calcium deficiency in the fruit, usually triggered by inconsistent watering — not lack of calcium in soil.

    • Prevention: Water deeply and consistently. Mulch 2-3" to retain moisture. Avoid alternating wet/dry cycles.
    • Don't bother with: Calcium sprays, eggshells, or Tums — the issue is water uptake, not soil calcium.
    • Brandywine is prone because its large fruit has high calcium demand during rapid growth.
    Cracking / Splitting

    Happens when heavy rain follows a dry spell — the inside of the fruit grows faster than the skin can stretch.

    • Concentric cracks: Rings around the stem. Usually heals, still edible.
    • Radial cracks: Lines from stem to bottom. Pick immediately, more prone to rot.
    • Prevention: Consistent watering (1-1.5" per week), thick mulch, pick fruit at first sign of cracking.
    For Decatur summer: Set up soaker hoses or drip irrigation on a timer. Brandywine + Georgia summer thunderstorms = cracking risk. Mulch heavily with your straw.

Learn More

  • Watch: Cherokee Purple — heirloom tomato reviewSearch YouTube
  • Learn: Why dark tomatoes have more antioxidants (anthocyanins) ↓ details

    Cherokee Purple's dark color comes from anthocyanins — the same pigments in blueberries, red wine, and purple cabbage. These are powerful antioxidants linked to reduced inflammation and heart health.

    • Chlorophyll retention: Dark tomatoes also retain more green chlorophyll even when ripe, which mixes with red lycopene to create the purple-brown color
    • Higher lycopene: Dark varieties often have 30-40% more lycopene than standard red tomatoes
    • Flavor connection: The complex, smoky flavor comes from higher sugar-to-acid balance plus volatile compounds not found in standard varieties
    Judging ripeness is harder on dark tomatoes — squeeze gently. A ripe Cherokee Purple yields slightly to pressure and feels heavy. The shoulders will stay green/dark even when ripe. Don't wait for uniform color — you'll overripen.

  • Learn: Making sweet jam from cherry tomatoes ↓ details

    Yellow Pear makes exceptional jam because of its high sugar, low acid profile. The resulting jam is a golden-amber color with a sweet, almost tropical flavor.

    Basic Yellow Pear Jam Recipe
    • 4 cups Yellow Pear tomatoes, halved
    • 1.5 cups sugar
    • 2 tbsp lemon juice (needed for gel + safety)
    • 1 packet pectin (low-sugar variety works best)
    • Optional: vanilla bean, ginger, or cinnamon

    Cook tomatoes down 20-30 min until soft, add sugar + lemon + pectin, boil 1 min, pour into jars. Makes ~3 half-pint jars.

    Cherry tomatoes don't need peeling — the skins dissolve when cooked. If you want smooth jam, hit it with an immersion blender before adding pectin.

Paste Tomato · Determinate · Open-Pollinated

Roma

Growing Profile

  • Days to maturity: 75-80 days from transplant
  • Plant type: Determinate — grows to fixed height, sets all fruit in a concentrated 4–6 week window, then stops
  • Fruit shape: Plum-shaped, 2–3 oz
  • Flesh: Meaty, dense, thick walls, few seeds, low moisture
  • Best use: Jam, sauce, paste, canning — ideal for batch processing
  • Yield: ~25–50 lbs per plant; 6 plants = 150–300 lbs in one harvest window
  • Similar to: San Marzano, Amish Paste (but determinate = concentrated harvest)

Determinate Advantage

  • No sucker removal needed — every flower is a potential fruit
  • No flower pinching — let all blooms set
  • Concentrated harvest — all fruit ripens in a 4–6 week window (early–mid July in Zone 8a)
  • Batch jam production — harvest once, process once, done for the season

Seed Saving

Open-Pollinated — Save seeds!

When: Fully red, slightly soft. How: Fermentation method — see illustrated guide under Rainbow above. Select from the meatiest, most uniform fruits for next season's stock.

Learn More

  • Watch: Roma tomato growing guideSearch YouTube
  • Learn: Making and canning tomato jam from paste tomatoes ↓ details

    Paste tomatoes like Roma are ideal for jam because they have thick walls, few seeds, and low water content. Less cook-down time = more flavor and better texture.

    Paste Tomato Jam (from Roma)
    • 3 lbs paste tomatoes, roughly chopped (no need to peel)
    • 1.25 cups sugar
    • 2 tbsp lemon juice
    • 1 tsp salt
    • Flavor options: smoked paprika, red pepper flakes, balsamic vinegar, fresh basil

    Cook tomatoes until collapsed (~15 min). Add sugar, lemon, salt. Simmer 45-60 min stirring often until thick and jammy. It's done when it coats a spoon and a line drawn through it holds.

    Freezer vs. canning: For freezer jam, just cool and freeze in jars (leave headspace). For shelf-stable canning, process in a boiling water bath for 15 min (pints). Always add the lemon juice for safe pH.
    ▶ Watch: Roma tomato jam
  • Learn: Determinate vs. indeterminate tomatoes ↓ details
    Why Roma is determinate — and why that matters for jam
    • Determinate (Roma): Grows to a fixed height, flowers all at once, fruit ripens in a concentrated 4–6 week window, then the plant is done
    • Indeterminate (most other tomatoes): Keeps growing, flowering, and fruiting all season until frost
    • For jam production: Determinate is ideal — you get a massive harvest all at once for batch processing
    • No pruning needed: Don't remove suckers or flowers on determinates — every flower = more fruit
    Your 6 Roma plants will produce roughly 150–300 lbs of paste tomatoes in one concentrated harvest window around early–mid July. That's a serious jam operation — plan your canning supplies in advance!

  • Learn: Why peppers drop flowers in high heat (>90 F) ↓ details

    Peppers drop their flowers ("blossom drop") when daytime temps exceed 90°F or nighttime temps stay above 75°F. In Decatur, this happens most of July-August.

    What's happening

    High heat kills pollen viability. The flower opens, but pollen can't fertilize the ovule, so the plant aborts the flower to save energy. This is normal and temporary.

    What to do
    • Don't panic. Once temps drop below 90°F regularly (Sept in Decatur), flowers will set again and you'll get a fall flush of peppers.
    • Shade cloth: 30-40% shade cloth over peppers during peak heat can lower canopy temp 5-10°F.
    • Water consistently: Drought stress + heat = worse blossom drop.
    • Mulch heavily: Keeps roots cool. Your straw mulch will help.
    For your Bed B: Expect a July production dip. Focus on harvesting the early peppers (June), then let the plants ride through the heat for a strong September-October harvest.

    Pepper cross-pollination risk

    All your peppers (Cajun Belle, Orange Bell, Lunchbox, Jalapeño) are the same species: Capsicum annuum. They will cross-pollinate via bees and insects. This means saved seeds could produce unexpected heat levels or shapes.

    How to isolate for pure seed
    • Bagging method: Cover flower buds with small organza/tulle bags before they open. When the flower self-pollinates inside the bag, you get pure seed. Tag the bagged fruit with tape so you know which ones to save.
    • Distance: 300+ ft between varieties for natural isolation (not practical in most gardens)
    • Timing: Early-season flowers are less likely to be cross-pollinated (fewer bees active)
    What if they cross? The fruit you eat this year is unaffected. But next year's plants from saved seed could be a surprise — you might get a sweet pepper with jalapeño heat, or a jalapeño with no kick. If you're OK with surprises, just save from any fruit!

Seed Saving

Open-Pollinated — Easy to save!

When: Leave pods on the plant until brown, dry, and rattling (Sept-Oct). No isolation needed — beans self-pollinate.

Bean seed saving — the easiest method. No processing, just patience. Works for Blue Lake and Stringless Green.

Learn More

  • Watch: Growing bush beans from seed to harvestSearch YouTube
  • Learn: Saving bean seeds — the easiest seed to save ↓ details

    Beans are the perfect beginner seed-saving crop. They self-pollinate before the flower even opens, so cross-pollination is nearly impossible.

    Step by step
    • 1. Mark plants: Choose 5-6 healthy plants from your best-producing area. Tie a ribbon on them.
    • 2. Stop picking: Let ALL pods on marked plants mature fully. Don't pick any for eating.
    • 3. Wait: Pods will turn yellow, then brown, then papery and dry. They'll rattle when shaken (Sept-Oct).
    • 4. Harvest: Pick dry pods. If rain threatens, pick and finish drying indoors.
    • 5. Shell: Crack open pods, shake out beans. Spread on a plate for 1-2 more weeks of drying indoors.
    • 6. Store: Paper envelope or glass jar in a cool, dark place. Viable 3-4 years.
    How many to save: One Blue Lake plant produces 20-30 pods. If each pod has 5-6 seeds, that's 100-180 seeds from one plant. Save from 5 plants and you'll have enough to plant all of Bed C and share with friends.
  • Understand: Succession planting for continuous harvests ↓ details

    Bush beans produce heavily for about 3 weeks, then decline. Without succession sowing, you get one big harvest and then nothing.

    Your succession schedule for Beds B & C
    • Sow 1: Mid-April (after last frost ~Apr 15)
    • Sow 2: Early May (+2-3 weeks)
    • Sow 3: Late May (+2-3 weeks)
    • Sow 4 (optional): Mid-June — last sowing before summer heat slows production
    • Fall sow: Late August/early September for a fall harvest

    Each sowing overlaps with the previous one's decline, creating a continuous harvest from late May through July, then again in October.

    Practical tip: You don't need separate bed space. Sow 2 goes in the gaps between Sow 1 plants, or in a different strip of the bed. By the time Sow 3 goes in, Sow 1 is done and you can pull those plants and replant the space.

    Okra seed saving is dead simple. The same pods you eat, if left on the plant, dry into perfect seed pods.

    How to save
    • Let 3-4 pods per plant grow past eating size (they'll get woody and inedible — that's correct)

  • Understand: Soaking okra seeds to break dormancy ↓ details

    Okra seeds have a hard, waterproof seed coat. Without treatment, germination is slow and spotty (only 50-60% may sprout).

    Three methods (pick one)
    • Soak overnight (easiest): Drop seeds in warm water for 12-24 hours. Plant immediately after. Germination jumps to 80-90%.
    • Nick the seed coat: Use nail clippers to barely chip the seed coat opposite the "eye." This lets water in. Plant same day.
    • Sandpaper scuff: Rub seeds on fine sandpaper to thin the coat. Good for large batches.
    For your April planting: Soak seeds the night before you plan to sow. Plant 1" deep, 12-18" apart. Soil must be 65°F+ — check with a soil thermometer. In Decatur, early April soil should be warm enough.

    Bitterness in cucumbers comes from cucurbitacin, a compound that increases when the plant is stressed.

    Stress triggers
    • Inconsistent watering — the #1 cause. Cucumbers need 1-1.5" per week minimum, more in summer heat.
    • High heat — above 95°F, plants produce more cucurbitacin
    • Too little water: Even one dry spell can trigger bitterness in fruit forming during that period
    • Overmature fruit: Old, yellowing cucumbers get bitter. Pick frequently!
    Prevention
    • Drip irrigation or soaker hose on a timer
    • Heavy mulch to retain moisture
    • Pick slicers at 6-8", picklers at 2-4" — don't let them get big
    • If one is bitter: peel from blossom end toward stem (bitterness concentrates at the stem end)

Challenge: Onions are biennial — they produce seed in their 2nd year. How: Save the best bulbs after harvest, store over winter, replant in spring. Learn More

  • Watch: Growing sweet onions in the SouthSearch YouTube
  • Learn: Short-day vs. long-day onions (and why it matters in GA) ↓ details

    Onions form bulbs in response to day length, not temperature or age. The wrong type literally won't bulb in your climate.

    The three types
    • Short-day (10-12 hrs): Bulb when days are 10-12 hours. This is what you need in Georgia. Plant in fall (Oct-Nov) or late winter. Harvest late spring/early summer. Examples: Texas 1015, Vidalia, Yellow Granex.
    • Long-day (14-16 hrs): Need 14+ hour days to bulb. Won't work in the South — Decatur never gets 14+ hours of daylight. These are for northern states (Zone 5 and above).
    • Day-neutral (12-14 hrs): Bulb regardless of day length. Work anywhere but not as sweet as short-day varieties. Examples: Candy, Cabernet.
    For Decatur, GA (Zone 8a): Always buy short-day varieties. If a label just says "onion" without specifying, check the variety name. Vidalia-type onions are short-day and bred specifically for Georgia.

Learn More

  • Watch: Growing garlic from planting to harvestSearch YouTube
  • Learn: Softneck vs. hardneck garlic for Zone 8a ↓ details
    Two types of garlic
    • Softneck: Best for the South. No flower stalk (scape). Stores 6-9 months. Smaller cloves but more of them (12-20 per bulb). Easy to braid. Varieties: Inchelium Red, California Early, Silverskin.
    • Hardneck: Prefers cold winters. Produces a flower stalk (scape) in spring — edible and delicious! Larger cloves but fewer (6-10 per bulb). Stores only 3-5 months. Varieties: Music, German Extra Hardy, Purple Stripe.
    For Decatur: Softneck is the safer bet — guaranteed to bulb well. But hardneck varieties like Music and Chesnok Red also work in Zone 8a if planted early (Oct). The bonus: you get garlic scapes in April, which are a delicacy — great in stir-fry and pesto.
  • Learn: Curing and storing garlic for 6+ months ↓ details
    Why curing matters

    Fresh-dug garlic has too much moisture to store. Curing dries the outer wrappers and neck, sealing the cloves inside and preventing rot.

    How to cure
    • Harvest when lower 1/3 of leaves are brown (late May-June in Decatur)
    • Don't wash. Gently brush off dirt.
    • Hang or lay flat in a warm (80°F), dry, well-ventilated spot. Covered porch, garage, or shed works. NOT in direct sun.
    • Wait 3-4 weeks. Wrappers should be papery, necks dry, roots wiry.
    • Trim: Cut stems to 1" above bulb. Trim roots close.
    • Store: Cool (55-65°F), dry, dark place with airflow. Mesh bags or open baskets. Do NOT refrigerate (triggers sprouting).
    Storage life: Properly cured softneck garlic keeps 6-9 months (June harvest lasts through February). Hardneck keeps 3-5 months. Remember to save your biggest, best bulbs for replanting in October!

Key Production Practices 12 techniques

1Consistent Deep Watering

Irregular watering is the #1 cause of lost tomato production.

  • Aim for 1–2″ per week, deep soaks rather than shallow frequent watering
  • Drip irrigation is highly recommended
  • Uneven moisture causes blossom end rot and fruit cracking

2Calcium Supplementation

  • Crush eggshells and mix into planting hole
  • OR use gypsum at bloom time
  • Prevents blossom end rot, which can destroy 30%+ of yield

3Feeding Schedule

  • Use balanced 10-10-10 fertilizer at planting
  • Switch to higher-phosphorus/potassium formula (5-10-10 or tomato-specific) once flowering begins
  • Feed every 2–3 weeks during growing season
  • Excess nitrogen produces lots of leaves but few tomatoes

4Heavy Mulching

  • 2–3″ of straw mulch (EZ Straw works well)
  • Retains moisture, regulates soil temperature
  • Prevents soil splash (disease prevention)
  • Suppresses weeds

5Pruning Indeterminates (NOT Roma)

  • On slicing/heirloom plants, remove suckers (shoots in leaf axils) to direct energy to fruit
  • Remove lower leaves touching the ground to prevent disease spread
  • Do NOT prune determinate Roma plants — every flower is a potential fruit

6Hand-Pollination

  • Tomatoes are self-pollinating but need vibration
  • On calm mornings, gently shake flower clusters or tap stakes
  • Some gardeners use an electric toothbrush on flower stems
  • Increases fruit set dramatically

7Magnesium (Epsom Salt)

  • 1 tbsp per plant at transplant
  • Repeat at first fruit set
  • Supports chlorophyll production and fruit development

8First Flower Cluster Removal

Young plants only.

  • Pinching the very first flowers on young plants encourages stronger root/plant development before fruiting
  • Already a current practice — continue

9Succession Planting

Indeterminates only.

  • Plant a second wave of indeterminate starts in mid-June
  • Provides late summer/fall harvest after first wave slows

10Companion Planting

  • Basil (already implemented across bags and beds)
  • Marigolds, nasturtiums also help
  • Avoid planting near brassicas or fennel

11Disease Prevention

  • Maintain good airflow between plants
  • Remove yellowing leaves promptly
  • Water at soil level, not on foliage
  • Apply copper spray if early blight appears

12Foliar Feeding

  • Weak seaweed/kelp spray every 2 weeks
  • Provides micronutrient boost that soil feeding can miss

Method

  1. Roughly chop tomatoes, purée briefly with salt.

Freezing Strategy Production notes
Roma's determinate nature dumps 150–300 lbs in July all at once. Freeze whole or chopped tomatoes (no blanching needed for jam) to pace production across fall and winter. Freezing actually breaks down cell walls and speeds reduction cooking. Heirloom surplus can also be frozen and blended into any jam or bloody mary batch later.
